Próspera

A private charter city on the island of Roatán, Honduras, operating under its own legal code, tax structure, and governance system. Near-zero corporate taxes, zero capital gains, private arbitration, and a digital e-residency program. Its constitutional status is disputed following Honduras's 2022 repeal of the ZEDE law; a $10.775 billion ICSID arbitration case is ongoing while the zone continues to operate.

Why it matters

  • Próspera is the most advanced operational test of jurisdictional entrepreneurship to date. A functioning zone with its own legal code, private arbitration system, tax structure, and physical infrastructure — most experiments in this space remain theoretical. This one is operating.
  • The $10.775 billion ICSID case under CAFTA-DR is the highest-stakes legal test yet of whether startup societies can enforce contractual protections against political reversal by a host nation. The outcome will set a precedent for every future experiment that operates within an existing state.
  • Despite its 50-year stability clause, Próspera's existence remains significantly dependent on host-nation political conditions. The shift from Castro to Asfura changed the climate overnight — underscoring why the movement needs not just contractual protections, but ultimately exit rights and full sovereignty.

Frequently Asked

A ZEDE (Zona de Empleo y Desarrollo Económico) is a special autonomous jurisdiction created by Honduras's 2013 constitutional amendments, with its own legal code, tax system, and governance structure independent of Honduran national administration. Three were established before the law was repealed in 2022. Honduras's Supreme Court declared all ZEDEs retroactively unconstitutional in September 2024; that ruling is disputed by Próspera in ongoing ICSID arbitration.

Contested. Honduras repealed the ZEDE law in April 2022 and the Supreme Court declared all ZEDEs unconstitutional in September 2024. Próspera disputes retroactive application, citing a 50-year contractual stability agreement, and continues to operate on Roatán. An ICSID arbitration case under CAFTA-DR survived preliminary objections in February 2025. The political environment improved in early 2026 when President Asfura, more business-friendly toward ZEDEs, took office.

There are three tiers. A Visitor Pass (free) allows unlimited daily access and 30 overnight stays per year. e-Residency ($130/year) provides digital identity and business formation rights with up to 30 days physical presence, designed for remote entrepreneurs. Full Residency ($130 plus mandatory insurance) grants full-time access and all business rights.

Apply at eprospera.hn. Requires identity verification, a criminal background check, and signing the "Agreement of Coexistence." Processing typically takes one to three days. Próspera residency does not replace Honduran immigration status.

In December 2022, Honduras Próspera Inc. filed ICSID Case No. ARB/23/2 under CAFTA-DR, claiming the repeal of the ZEDE law constituted unlawful expropriation. The $10.775 billion figure represents claimed future lost returns and equals roughly one-third of Honduras's annual GDP. Critics have called it an overreach of investor-state dispute mechanisms. The case is at the merits phase as of early 2026.

The main criticisms are: sovereignty (a Delaware-incorporated company exercises government-like powers over Honduran territory without meaningful democratic oversight); indigenous land rights (the fishing village of Crawfish Rock was not properly consulted, and water access disputes emerged in 2019); regulatory risk (Minicircle's unregulated gene therapy trials showed how the permissive environment can be misused); and ISDS overreach (a $10.775 billion claim against a country whose annual budget is roughly $16 billion).
